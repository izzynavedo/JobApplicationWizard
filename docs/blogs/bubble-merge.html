What This Is

Job Application Wizard is a macOS app for tracking job applications. Its AI assistant, Cuttle, is rendered as an animated blob: three overlapping circular fins with additive RGB color mixing. Where all three fins overlap, the colors add to white. (The previous post covers how that logo was built from the wave equation up.)

The next feature is parallel agents: Cuttle can spawn sub-agents that work on different parts of a complex task simultaneously. When those subtasks finish, the blobs need to rejoin. That means merging two animated blobs into one.

A crossfade would be cheap and forgettable. A snap-cut would be confusing. I wanted something that felt physical: resistance, deformation, release. Like two soap bubbles touching and coalescing.

Why Soap Bubbles

Real bubble coalescence has three phases with dramatically different timescales:

  1. Resistance and bulging (~100ms): surface tension resists coalescence. The contact region flattens; the far sides bulge outward as internal pressure redistributes. High-frequency tremor vibrates the contact film.
  2. Film rupture (~1ms): the thin film between the bubbles snaps. Surface tension retracts the film edge at meters per second. A brief brightness flash at the rupture point.
  3. Settling (~500ms): the merged bubble oscillates as a damped harmonic system, overshooting in alternating axes before converging to a sphere.

The key insight: three orders of magnitude separate these timescales. Resistance is slow and tense; rupture is instant; settling is a gentle ring-down. That contrast is what makes coalescence feel physical rather than procedural.

For animation, I exaggerated these ratios for readability (0.4s resist, 0.08s snap, 0.8s settle) while preserving the essential shape: the resist phase is 5× the snap. Try flattening these in the sliders above and watch the physicality disappear.

The Architecture

The merge animation reuses JitterCircle.blobPath(), the same static function that renders every idle Cuttle blob. The key addition: a deformation closure that lets the merge view inject per-angle radial offsets without changing the base renderer. 

static func blobPath(
    center: CGPoint,
    bodyRadius: Double,
    amplitude: Double,
    elapsed: Double,
    rotation: Double,
    deformation: ((Double, CGPoint) -> Double)? = nil
) -> Path

This means the idle blob, the thinking-pulse blob, and the merging blob all share one renderer. The merge view never builds its own path math; it just provides a closure that says "at this angle, push the surface inward (flatten) or outward (bulge) by this much."

During the resist phase, the deformation closure uses cosine weighting to concentrate deformation on the contact hemisphere: 

let flatten = -resistStrength * cos(angle - angleToPeer)
let tremor = tremorAmp * sin(tremorFreq * elapsed) * max(0, cos(angle - angleToPeer))
return flatten + tremor

The cos(angle - angleToPeer) term is positive on the contact side and negative on the far side. Multiplying tremor by max(0, cos(...)) ensures only the contact face vibrates; the far side bulges smoothly.

After the snap, a damped spring drives the settling oscillation: 

let scaleX = 1.0 + 0.15 * exp(-5 * t) * cos(6 * .pi * t)
let scaleY = 1.0 - 0.15 * exp(-5 * t) * cos(6 * .pi * t)

The horizontal and vertical scales oscillate in antiphase: when the blob stretches wide, it compresses vertically, and vice versa. The exponential decay produces about three visible cycles before the blob reads as settled.

The Deformation Sign Problem

The hardest bug was invisible in the math. When the flatten coefficient exceeded ~40% of the radius, the cosine-weighted deformation pushed the contact surface past the center of the blob, creating a concavity. Two concavities facing each other produced a pinched, inverted shape:

Flatten at 65%: the contact side inverts, creating opposing concavities.

The fix was not "less deformation." It was separating flatten from bulge directionally. Flatten uses max(0, cos(angle)): it only pushes inward on the contact hemisphere, never inverts. Bulge uses max(0, -cos(angle)): it only pushes outward on the far hemisphere. The deformation budget goes to the far-side bulge and the contact-side tremor, not the flatten depth.

This is a general principle for radial deformation: clamp directionally, not by magnitude. Reducing the coefficient does not prevent inversion at all values; clamping to a half-space does.

Why Sliders, Not Code

The animation has 15 interdependent parameters. Changing the resist duration affects how much deformation is visible. Changing the flatten strength affects how tight the contact gap needs to be. Changing the settle wobble affects whether the merged blob feels "alive" or "overshooting."

Tuning these by editing constants and recompiling does not work. The feedback loop is too slow and the parameter interactions are nonlinear. Instead, I built a standalone demo app with a looping merge animation and a slider for every dependent variable, grouped by phase.

The slider demo paid for itself in the first minute. Parameters that seemed reasonable in isolation (50% flatten, 40% bulge) produced opposing concavities when combined. Values that felt too subtle alone (2% tremor amplitude) became essential for selling the resist phase as physical rather than static. The right values were found by watching, not by calculating.

The interactive sliders above are the same tool, ported to the browser. Drag them; the animation restarts on every change.

What I Learned

Timescale contrast is everything. The animation feels physical because the resist phase is 5× the snap. If all phases have equal duration, it reads as a morph, not a coalescence. Try setting all four timing sliders to the same value above.

Clamp directionally, not by magnitude. Reducing a coefficient prevents artifacts at the current value but not at all values. Clamping to a half-space (flatten only inward, bulge only outward) eliminates the entire class of sign-inversion bugs.

Build the tuning tool first. Twelve parameters interacting nonlinearly cannot be tuned by reading code. A slider panel with a looping preview is the fastest path to good-feeling animation.

Reuse the idle renderer. By making blobPath() static with a deformation hook, the merge animation produces the exact same visual language as the idle blob. No shader swap, no separate renderer. The blob looks like the same creature because it is the same creature, just with different inputs.

The complete merge animation is about 250 lines of Swift. Same tools as the logo: Canvas, Path, sin(), exp(), and a TimelineView. Every animation on this page is the same math, ported to HTML5 Canvas.

Iteration Log

The final parameters were reached through five rounds of visual feedback. This section shows each iteration frozen at peak deformation so the differences are visible. I built this animation collaboratively with Claude Code, using a prompt-driven feedback loop: describe what's wrong visually, get a new build, evaluate, repeat.

Round 1: First build

Five-phase state machine with hardcoded durations. Flatten at 5% of radius; no far-side bulge; no settling shrink-back. The deformation was invisible at 48px, and the merged blob stayed permanently oversized.

Round 2: Visible deformation + shrink-back

Flatten to 20%, bulge to 12%, tremor added. Post-merge blob now starts at √2× radius and shrinks back over 1.8s. The animation reads as "something happened" for the first time.

Round 3: Tighter gap

Flatten to 35%, bulge to 25%. Pressed gap tightened from 1.7r to 1.2r so the blobs visibly press against each other. Now the squish is unmistakable.

Round 4: The sign problem

Added pressure-driven deformation that ramped with proximity (up to 2.5× at peak squeeze). Flatten hit 65%, inverted, created opposing concavities. Fix: clamped flatten to the contact hemisphere only, moved deformation energy to far-side bulge.

Each iteration frozen at peak resist phase for comparison.

Round 5: Slider tuning

Built a standalone demo app with 15 sliders. Spent ten minutes dragging and watching. Converged on the final values: 20% flatten, 18% bulge, 2% tremor at 8Hz, 0.08s snap with 60% flash, damped spring settle at 30% wobble decaying over 3 cycles.
